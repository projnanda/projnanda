## Understanding NANDA and the Agentic Web

### What is NANDA?

**NANDA** stands for **Networked AI Agents in Decentralized Architecture**. It's a global research and infrastructure initiative under the Foundation for Agentic Networks (FAN) â€” a non-profit organization dedicated to building the foundation for the Open Agentic Web.

If the early Internet connected computers, and the Web connected people, the **Agentic Web will connect autonomous AI agents** â€” intelligent software entities that can act, learn, and collaborate on behalf of humans and organizations.

### Why NANDA Matters

#### The Early Promise of the Internet
When the Internet first emerged, it carried an incredible promise: open communication, free exchange of ideas, and democratized access to knowledge.

#### The Centralization Problem
Over time, that openness began to narrow:
- A small number of companies began to control much of the world's information flow
- Algorithms optimized for engagement created echo chambers and fueled polarization
- Design patterns that kept people scrolling contributed to growing mental health issues

**NANDA exists to avoid repeating those same mistakes as we transition into the next digital era.**

### The Agentic Web: The Next Internet

The Agentic Web represents a shift from human-operated systems to networks where AI agents act on our behalf. Imagine:

- A student's AI assistant coordinating with a professor's agent to schedule meetings
- A local environmental agent analyzing pollution data and reporting it directly to a city dashboard
- Agents representing organizations negotiating contracts, managing logistics, or even co-writing research papers

These agents won't just operate within single apps or platforms; they'll move fluidly across the entire Internet. But for that to work, they need a new kind of infrastructureâ€”something more flexible, secure, and transparent than today's systems.

**That's where NANDA comes in.**

### What NANDA Builds

#### The NANDA Index
A new kind of registry that allows AI agents to be discovered, verified, and trusted. Think of it as a **decentralized phonebook for the Agentic Web**â€”one that lets agents find each other, verify credentials, and exchange information safely.

Unlike centralized systems like DNS or corporate app stores, the NANDA Index is open and lightweight. It uses **AgentFacts**, a metadata schema that allows each agent to describe its capabilities, trust level, and connections in a verifiable way.

#### Decentralization and Trust
NANDA's architecture embraces decentralization to prevent control from consolidating in a few hands. Multiple organizations, developers, and communities can host their own NANDA registries, creating a network of interoperable systems rather than a single gatekeeper.

It also integrates concepts from **Zero Trust security**â€”every agent must verify another's identity and capabilities before interaction.

#### Interoperability Across Platforms
NANDA research shows how its system can interoperate with existing frameworks like Anthropic's MCP, Google's Agent-to-Agent (A2A), and Microsoft's NLWeb, bridging these separate ecosystems into a single, open web of agents.

NANDA research is organized into three broad tracks that guide ambassador activities:

**Focus:** How social systems, governance, and values evolve with the rise of agentic technologies.

**Example Topics:**
- Ethics and Governance
- Education and Literacy
- Public Trust and Accountability

**Example Questions:**
- How might agentic systems change the relationship between citizens and institutions?
- What ethical frameworks best support human agency in AI-mediated decision-making?
- How should governments balance innovation and regulation in the Agentic Web?

**Suggested Activities:**

- Partner with local coding clubs or research groups to explore agentic simulations

## Event Guidelines

### Purpose of NANDA Events

NANDA events are designed to foster dialogue, exploration, and collaboration around the ideas of the Agentic Web. Events should always reflect NANDA's values:

- Openness and accessibility
- Respect for diverse perspectives
- Commitment to ethical technology
- Collaboration over competition

### Planning and Approval

Before planning an event:

1. Submit a short proposal to your NANDA coordinator outlining the event's theme, goals, and estimated attendance
2. Confirm alignment with NANDA's mission and code of conduct
3. **Avoid using the MIT or MIT Media Lab logos or branding.** When referencing the project, use "NANDA Student Ambassador Program" or "Project NANDA"
4. Budget responsibly and keep clear records of all expenses

### Inclusivity and Respect

Guidelines for conduct:
- Foster inclusive participation
- Be mindful of cultural and accessibility needs
- Handle disagreements constructively
- Avoid political or commercial endorsements

**Remember:** You are a facilitator of dialogue, not an arbiter of truth.

### Event Documentation

After each event, please submit:
- Event name and date
- Location or platform
- Number of attendees (approximate)
- Audience type
- Main topics or insights discussed
- Photos or materials (with consent)

### Event Ideas

- **Discussion Circles:** Small group discussions on digital identity, explainable AI, or ethics
- **Workshops:** Interactive sessions teaching participants to prototype agentic systems
- **Community Showcases:** Students present creative interpretations of digital agency
- **Cross-disciplinary Panels:** Conversations between technologists, ethicists, and artists

### Safety, Privacy, and Consent

- Protect participant privacy
- Comply with local school/university policies
- Avoid collecting personal data unless necessary
- Ensure all participants feel welcome and safe
